The People of Mongolia

Nomadic warriors, horsemen, and keepers of the eternal flame

🐎

Nomadic Life

Nearly 30% of Mongolians still live as nomads, moving with their herds across the vast steppe. The horse is sacred — there are more horses than people in Mongolia.

🏠

The Ger

The traditional Mongolian dwelling — a circular felt tent that can be assembled in under an hour. It faces south, and the door always opens to the sun.

🤼

Three Manly Skills

Every Mongolian warrior mastered wrestling, archery, and horsemanship — the three games of Naadam festival celebrated since the time of Genghis Khan.

🎵

Throat Singing

Khoomei — the ancient art of producing multiple pitches simultaneously. A single singer can sound like an entire orchestra, mimicking wind and nature.

🙏

Tengrism & Buddhism

Mongolians worshipped the Eternal Blue Sky (Тэнгэр) long before Buddhism arrived. Today both beliefs intertwine in daily Mongolian spiritual life.

🍖

Food & Hospitality

Refusing food in a Mongolian ger is deeply offensive. Airag (fermented mare's milk), Buuz (steamed dumplings), and mutton are the heart of Mongolian cuisine.

The Ancient Mongolian Script

One of the few vertical scripts still in use today — born in the 13th century

1204 AD

Birth of the Script

Genghis Khan ordered the creation of a writing system after capturing the Uyghur scribe Tatar-Tonga. The Uyghur script was adapted to write the Mongolian language for the first time.

ᠮᠣᠩᠭᠣᠯ
1227 AD

The Secret History

The Secret History of the Mongols — the oldest surviving Mongolian literary work — was written in this script. It recorded the life of Genghis Khan and the origins of the Mongol Empire.

ᠨᠢᠭᠤᠴᠠ
1269 AD

Phags-pa Script

Kublai Khan commissioned the Tibetan monk Drogön Chögyal Phagpa to create a new unified script for the entire Mongol Empire. It was used officially but never replaced the traditional script.

ᠴᠢᠩᠭᠢᠰ
1941 AD

Cyrillic Replaces the Script

Under Soviet influence, Mongolia adopted the Cyrillic alphabet. The ancient vertical script was banned for decades — nearly erasing a 700-year-old writing tradition from daily life.

ᠪᠢᠴᠢᠭ
1990 AD

The Revival

After Mongolia's democratic revolution, the traditional script was restored. Today it appears on government documents, currency, and monuments — and is taught in schools again.

ᠤᠯᠤᠰ
2025 AD

Living Script Today

Mongolia officially made the traditional script co-official alongside Cyrillic. It is one of the only vertical scripts still actively used in the world today — written top to bottom, left to right.

ᠬᠠᠭᠠᠨ
↕️ Written vertically — top to bottom
→ Columns read left to right
🔤 35 letters — vowels and consonants
📜 800+ years of continuous use

Genghis Khan — The Universal Ruler

From a boy left to die on the steppe to the conqueror of half the world

ᠴᠢᠩᠭᠢᠰ ᠬᠠᠭᠠᠨ

"I am the punishment of God. If you had not committed great sins, God would not have sent a punishment like me upon you."

— Genghis Khan
1162 AD

Born as Temujin

Born near the Onon River, he was named Temujin after a Tatar chief his father had captured. He was born clutching a blood clot — seen as a sign of destiny.

ᠲᠡᠮᠦᠵᠢᠨ
1178 AD

Kidnapped & Enslaved

His father was poisoned when Temujin was 9. He was later kidnapped by a rival clan and forced to wear a wooden collar as a slave before escaping into the wilderness.

ᠨᠢᠭᠤᠴᠠ
1186 AD

First Khan

Through alliances, courage, and brilliant strategy, Temujin united his own tribe and was elected Khan — leader — for the first time. His rise had begun.

ᠬᠠᠭᠠᠨ
1206 AD

Genghis Khan — Universal Ruler

At the great Kurultai assembly on the banks of the Onon River, all Mongol tribes united under one banner. Temujin was proclaimed Genghis Khan — ruler of all people.

ᠮᠣᠩᠭᠣᠯ
1215 AD

Beijing Falls

Genghis Khan conquered the Jin Dynasty capital — modern day Beijing. The Mongol army was unstoppable, using advanced siege weapons and brilliant military tactics.

ᠪᠡᠭᠡᠵᠢᠩ
1219 AD

The Silk Road Opens

After conquering Central Asia, Genghis Khan united the Silk Road under one empire — creating the largest free trade zone in history. Merchants could travel safely from China to Europe.

ᠲᠥᠷᠥ
1227 AD

Death & Legacy

Genghis Khan died aged 65 — his burial site remains unknown to this day, by his own request. He left behind the largest contiguous empire in history covering 24 million km².

ᠡᠵᠡᠨ
24M km² at empire's peak
40M people under Mongol rule
16M living descendants today
500+ years of Mongol influence
